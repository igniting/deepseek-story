DeepSeek V4: A View from the Twelfth Floor

A fictional account of 18 months inside a lab in Yuhang
April 2026~40 min read
I

The Inheritance

April 24, 2026. The light comes up over Yuhang the way it comes up most mornings in late April, which is to say slowly and at a low angle, with a haze on the canal that the locals call yan and the meteorologists call particulate, both of them right in their own way. My alarm goes off at 6:15. I lie in bed for another four minutes because the alarm is, in practice, a suggestion, and at 6:19 I get up and put water on for tea.

I live alone in a one-bedroom apartment on the eighth floor of a building in Liangzhu, about 20 minutes by metro from the office. The kitchen window faces east. On a clear morning I can see the low ridge of hills that separates Hangzhou from the Tianmu range; today is hazy and I can see the building across the courtyard and not much else. I drink the tea standing at the counter because I have not yet committed to the day.

The kettle clicks off and I think, as I have thought every morning for the last six weeks, today is the day, and then, more honestly, today is one of the days it might be. The launch window has been moving for two months. We froze the model in the last week of March. Post-training was finalized on April 8. The internal evaluations finished on the 14th. The technical report went through its final pass on the 19th, with three of us in a small conference room arguing about a single sentence in the post-training section for 80 minutes, until Su Yutong said just leave it and we left it. The Hugging Face repository has been staged since Sunday. The communications team has been ready since Monday. The Huawei team since Tuesday. We have been waiting, since Tuesday, for a decision none of us is responsible for and all of us are anticipating. I have had dreams about the launch in which I open the repository on my phone and the model card is empty.

I take the metro at 7:20. The Number Three line, west to Future Sci-Tech City. At 7:20 it is full but not crushed. I put my earphones in and listen to a podcast about the An Lushan Rebellion that I do not absorb very much of. I am thinking about whether the launch will hold today and whether, if it holds, the press will read the technical report or only the model cards and whether, if they read the report, they will understand what we have actually done, which is a separate question from whether they will praise it.

I get off at 8:11. I walk the 400 meters from the station to our building, past the noodle place that opens at 11, past the convenience store that has been our default snack supply since I joined, past the little planted area with the magnolia tree that has just finished flowering and is now putting out its first wide green leaves.

The twelfth floor at 8:15. Perhaps eleven people on the floor. The cleaning staff has been through. The desks are mostly clear except for the desks of the two people who slept here, one of whom is Chen Wei, on the conference room couch where he has been sleeping at least three nights a week since the second week of February.

My desk is in the second row from the window, on the side that gets afternoon light rather than morning. A monitor, a mechanical keyboard with switches my neighbors have asked me to consider replacing, a small cactus I have been incrementally killing for 14 months, a stained ceramic mug from a conference in Suzhou. I sit down. I wake the monitor. The screen comes up to the dashboard I left it on the night before. The progress bar on the upload terminal is at 97%. The bar is decorative. The actual upload finished overnight. What it is waiting for is the communications team's go-signal, which is what the entire twelfth floor is waiting for.

What is true about this floor, and what was true in October of 2023 when I joined, is that there is no clock-in. No formal performance reviews. No quarterly goals. No KPIs. One organizational level above me, occupied by one person. Most people walk out at six or seven. The reasoning is that nobody can sustain more than six to eight hours of high-quality output, so any system that demands more is a system that demands theatre. We are not performing the work. We are doing it.

The meeting rooms are in the middle of the floor, three in a row, with doors on both long sides that push open in either direction. In the 2024 interview that everyone read after R1, the founder told the journalist that the doors had been designed to leave room for serendipity — that the Transformer paper had, by some accounts, begun with a researcher overhearing a conversation through an open door. I have never decided whether I believed the story, but the doors had been there the whole time. They were doors.

At 8:40, Chen Wei comes out of the conference room with his hair pressed flat on one side and his glasses smudged. He is wearing the same black t-shirt he was wearing yesterday, which is the same one he was wearing the day before. He nods at me and goes to the kitchen. He comes back with two mugs and puts one on my desk without asking. He has correctly assumed it is the same tea as his — a green tea from a small farm near Longjing Village that his cousin works at.

Out yet? he says.

No.

What are they doing.

I think they're waiting on the Huawei livestream.

He nods. The Huawei communications team has scheduled a Bilibili livestream for 9:47, which they have been calling internally a "day-zero adaptation announcement," and our communications team has agreed not to flip the repository public until 40 minutes before the livestream. I learned this on Tuesday in a meeting that took 2.5 hours. Chen learned it the same morning and immediately stopped asking about it, because Chen has a low tolerance for coordination meetings and a high tolerance for being told what time things will happen.

Su Yutong is in the small conference room next to the cluster bay. She has been spending most of her mornings there since the post-training pipeline froze — sitting with the door closed, reading. I do not ask her what she is reading.

The floor fills up toward nine. People come off the elevators and go to their desks and start their machines and get tea and come back. Nobody is talking very much. Everyone knows the model is launching today and nobody has anything in particular to say about it. We finished the work two weeks ago. The launch is for other people.

At 9:06, the communications channel posts: go in 20 minutes. A researcher I have not spoken to in a month posts a single thumbs-up emoji and the channel goes quiet again. Through the glass wall of the small room I can see Su at the table, her laptop open, her head down. She has not looked up.

I think about the 18 months that have brought us to this morning. About the things I will never tell anyone outside this floor — the meetings, the people who left, the Huawei engineers on the cots, Chen at the whiteboard at one in the morning, the night in February I will come back to later — and I think about how those 18 months will, by lunchtime, become a single sentence in foreign press: DeepSeek released V4-Pro and V4-Flash on April 24, 2026. The sentence is true. Most of what is also true will not fit in the sentence.

At 9:20, the repository flips public. I refresh the page on my phone. The model card loads. I sit at my desk in a building in Yuhang and look at a webpage on a phone that is in my hand and feel, for a moment, nothing in particular. Then I feel something I do not have a clean word for, which is closest to relief but is not relief, and which I will, in the days afterward, decide was probably just the feeling of a long thing ending.

I message Chen: 它出去了.

He replies in seven seconds: 吃过早饭没.

I have not. I get up to find something to eat.

* * *
II

The Year of Pressure

January 20, 2025. R1 ships at 5 PM Beijing time, two weeks before Lunar New Year. I am at my parents' apartment in Hefei because my mother has made it clear, by way of three separate WeChat messages, that early is the new on-time. The apartment is the one I grew up in, on the seventh floor near the Anhui University campus. My father is in the kitchen making jiaozi. My mother is watching a variety show I do not recognize. I am at the small desk in my old bedroom, which still has the desk lamp I used for homework and a shelf of textbooks nobody has thought to throw away.

The release happens on time. I refresh the Hugging Face page once to confirm the weights are public. I refresh the GitHub page once to confirm the technical report is up. I close the laptop.

The news arrives in waves over the following five days, and the waves are not the waves we expected.

The first wave is professional. Hacker News. Reddit. Andrej Karpathy writes a post that becomes the post everyone is quoting by Wednesday. Yann LeCun writes a post about open source that becomes a different post everyone is quoting by Thursday. Jim Fan writes something kind. Several people I have never heard of write things that are not kind. By Tuesday afternoon I have stopped checking, because the substance has stabilized into a shape I can predict from the headlines.

The second wave is financial. NVIDIA opens on Monday morning in New York and falls 17%. By the close it has lost approximately $600 billion in market capitalization — the largest one-day decline by a single company in the history of the U.S. stock market. The Nasdaq closes down 3%. My father, who has not previously demonstrated any interest in my work, walks into the guest room and asks, in the polite voice he uses when he is trying not to sound impressed, what DeepSeek is. I tell him it is the company I work for. He nods and goes back to the kitchen and turns up the volume on the television, which my mother has tuned to CCTV-13. The anchor is reading from a prompter and saying our company name in the careful pronunciation reserved for words she has not said before. Shen-du Qiu-suo. My father comes back 10 minutes later and asks if I would like a beer. We do not normally have a beer together. I say yes.

The third wave is at home. The founder goes back to his hometown in Zhanjiang for the holiday, and his village hangs a red banner across the lane to his family's house: 热烈祝贺成为家乡的骄傲 — warm congratulations on becoming the pride of your hometown. A photograph appears on Weibo and is reposted approximately 400,000 times. By February, the Hangzhou municipal government has named DeepSeek as one of the 杭州六小龙 — the Six Little Dragons of Hangzhou. I learn we are one of the dragons from a SCMP article on the high-speed train back. I had not previously been aware that there was a list.

I return to the office on the twelfth, two days before most people, because I have used as many days of holiday as I plan to use this year. The floor is empty. I sit down. I wake the monitor. I open my email. There are 47 messages from headhunters. 47 over 23 days. I have never received a message from a headhunter before. The packages they describe are, by the standards I have grown up with, absurd. Two and a half times my pay. Three times. One of them offers me a position with a title I have never been offered and a number I read three times before closing the email without replying.

I do not reply to any of them. What I am thinking about, that morning, is what is in the inboxes of the more senior people on the floor.

What was in their inboxes was something larger than what was in mine.

The senior people — the eight or nine names on the R1 paper that the field knew — had begun receiving offers in the third week of January. By April, the offers had a shape. ByteDance was offering cash plus ByteDance options plus Doubao options. Tencent was offering cash plus Tencent options plus a structure I never fully understood. Xiaomi was offering a head-of-AI title with what the headhunters called full strategic latitude. The numbers, when they were reported in the press later in the year, would be reported as approximate and denied as overstated and would be, in the cases I have first-hand knowledge of, accurate. 100 million yuan a year. 80 million over four years. 60 million plus tail equity.

We could not match these numbers. DeepSeek had never raised outside capital. The company was wholly owned by High-Flyer, which was a partnership rather than a priced equity vehicle. The options the lab had begun issuing in late 2024 were tied to a strike price that nobody on the floor knew how to translate into an actual number. An option without a priced strike is not currency. It is a promise.

The first person to leave was Wei Haoran, who led the OCR series. He left over Lunar New Year for a position at a major tech company we were asked, in the all-hands the following Tuesday, not to name. Wang Bingxuan was next — he had been on the team that produced our first-generation language model. He left for Tencent in early February through a recruitment effort led by Yao Shunyu, who had become a person whose name was spoken on our floor with a particular tone. The tone was not hostile. It was the tone you use for a force of nature.

Then, on a Tuesday in February — I remember it was a Tuesday because I had a tooling review at three that I forgot to cancel and sat through anyway — Guo Daya told the team he was leaving.

Guo had been the architect of GRPO. His name was first author on the R1 paper. He was 31. He was, by every measure that mattered inside the lab, indispensable in the sense that nobody had done his job before him and nobody had been trained to do it after him. He had been working on the post-training pipeline that would eventually become the specialist-and-OPD architecture that Su Yutong would, six months later, lead to completion. He had been a quiet presence on the floor — soft-spoken, long-haired, the same gray hoodie for a week at a time — and he had been, in the small ways that someone is, a friend of mine. We had eaten lunch together. He had once helped me debug a kernel I had been stuck on for three days. I had once, in the spring of 2024, had a long conversation with him about a paper of Hinton's we had both found unconvincing, and we had stayed in the small kitchen until past nine drinking tea and arguing about it.

His email was three sentences. He had decided to take an opportunity at ByteDance Seed. He was deeply grateful. He would be available for the transition. The salary, when LatePost reported it the following week, was rumored near 100 million yuan a year, and ByteDance would deny the figure without denying the structure.

The all-hands was the next morning. The founder was in the room, in the back, against the wall, but he did not speak. The director of post-training spoke for about four minutes and said the things you say. There were no questions, because the questions everyone wanted to ask were questions you do not ask in an all-hands.

I remember three things from that meeting. Su Yutong, two rows ahead, did not turn around once, which was unusual because Su often turned around in meetings to make eye contact with people she wanted to talk to afterward. Chen Wei, to my right, was looking at his shoes. And I felt, briefly and shamefully, a small flicker of envy at a number I had not been offered, followed by a larger and more confusing irritation at Guo for timing his exit before V4 froze, followed by a residual affection that overwrote both of the previous feelings and that left me unsure of what I actually felt.

Su came to my desk that afternoon. She stood with one hand on the back of the empty chair and said, after a pause that was longer than it needed to be, 他做了他的选择. 我们做我们的. He made his choice. We make ours. Then she went back to her desk.

In the three months that followed, two more senior researchers left. By the end of March, the floor had lost five names the field knew. The lab still had 150 researchers. We were not gutted. The people who stayed had stayed. The people who left were not traitors for leaving. The calculus was different for each person, and was private, and was theirs. We came in. We went home at six. We came in again the next day.

Through all of this — the departures, the offers we were not accepting — the work that occupied the second half of 2025 and the first quarter of 2026 was trying to train V4 on Huawei's chips.

The press would later reduce this to the phrase adapted to run on Huawei chips, which is technically accurate and almost entirely misleading. What actually happened was harder.

Beijing had communicated, through the channels these things are communicated through, that the strategic preference was for V4 to train on Ascend. Not just to serve — to train. The U.S. export controls had hardened through 2025. The H100 supply that carried us through V3 was finite. Huawei had been pushing its Ascend 910C chips into training-cluster deployments. The political logic was clear. The technical logic was what we lived inside.

Two engineers from Huawei's Shenzhen team arrived at our office in October of 2025. They were given desks on the floor below, near the cluster bay. They were not given a project. They were given a problem: our 600B prototype training run on Ascend hardware was failing in ways we could not diagnose ourselves. One of them — I will call him Lin Zhao — moved into our small conference room within his first week, when it became clear the problem would outlast his hotel booking.

Lin Zhao was perhaps 35. Tsinghua electrical engineering, 8 years at Huawei. Tall and thin with the slightly hollow look of a person who has been on call too long. He spoke with a Beijing accent unusual on our largely southern floor and had a habit of stretching his neck every 15 minutes that I came to recognize as a tic born of long hours at a workstation. He smoked, which he had to do in the parking lot because the building did not permit it, and Chen — who does not smoke — began going down with him during the longer debug sessions, because Chen had figured out that the fastest way past a stuck problem was often a 15-minute walk away from the screen.

The problems were interconnect bandwidth that fell short of spec under sustained load; gaps in CANN, Huawei's CUDA equivalent, for operations our MoE routing logic depended on; and a stability problem in gradient accumulation that manifested as loss spikes we could not reliably reproduce. Lin Zhao patched kernels. Chen and three others rewrote our training loop to tolerate the interconnect quirks. We ran smaller-scale characterization runs — 70B, 100B, 200B. We found workarounds for some failures. The 600B run we attempted in late October failed at step 180,000 with a divergence we traced, after two weeks, to a kernel-level interaction between the load-balancing loss and a specific Ascend instruction that lacked the numerical precision the spec had implied. The fix required either a new kernel from Huawei — being written, not yet ready — or a redesign of our load-balancing scheme, which we were not willing to make this late.

A second 600B run in late November failed at step 240,000 for a different reason. The diagnosis took six days and produced a fix that worked for that specific run but did not generalize.

In early December, the founder convened a meeting. 15 people, including the Huawei engineers. I was not in the meeting. Su was. Chen was. The meeting lasted two hours. Chen told me the next morning that the decision had been made to move V4 training back to NVIDIA hardware and scope the Huawei work as serving-side adaptation. Su told me, a week later, that the founder had asked questions for most of the two hours and had, near the end, said one sentence everyone remembered: 先把模型做出来. Let's make the model first.

Lin Zhao and his colleague stayed until just before Lunar New Year, now working on inference-side adaptation. The work went better. By March, Huawei's Ascend 950 supernodes were giving us per-token inference latencies we could match against our NVIDIA numbers without embarrassment. I saw Lin Zhao once more, in a video call in late March. He had a haircut and looked, for the first time since I had met him, like a person who had slept.

What I will say about this period honestly is that it was not a triumph and it was not a defeat. We tried to train on Chinese chips and could not. We trained on NVIDIA chips. We served on Chinese chips, and in serving at production scale we accomplished something real that was not what we had originally set out to accomplish. The distinction matters. The distinction will, in most public retellings, be lost.

By the time the Huawei work had settled into its serving-side shape, it was January 2026. The model was not yet trained. The architecture was not finalized. Half the lab had spent the previous quarter on infrastructure work that would not ship in the first version of V4. We had lost five senior researchers. The ones who remained were tired. Outside, the news cycle had begun asking when DeepSeek would ship its next model, increasingly with a tone that suggested the people asking had stopped expecting an answer.

We had three months.

* * *
III

The Architecture

By the time we came back from Lunar New Year on February 16, we had three months until the launch window. We did not have a model. We had a set of architectural decisions that had survived the previous quarter and a set that had not.

The press accounts of V4 would treat the architecture as though it had been planned from the beginning, the way papers always make work look in retrospect. The architecture that shipped on April 24 was what emerged from 18 months of partial decisions, abandoned alternatives, and three particularly long arguments.

We had the inheritance from V3.2 — the DeepSeek Sparse Attention mechanism, the lightning indexer, the top-k token selection — built with the understanding that V4 would need it at a scale V3.2 was not yet ready for. Sebastian Raschka, in his post-V3.2 summary, had written that the release looked like an experimental rehearsal for a larger model. He had been correct, and several of us had read his post on the morning it appeared and had said nothing about it to each other.

We had MLA, which was approaching its useful limits. The KV cache compression it achieved was excellent up to a few hundred thousand tokens. At a million — the V4 target — the proportionality dominated the compression. We had the Muon optimizer, which converged faster than AdamW for our MoE configuration and which we had decided to use for the bulk of parameters, keeping AdamW for embeddings and the prediction head where it had a longer track record. We had MXFP4, the four-bit quantization-aware training scheme that halved expert memory compared to FP8 and that mapped cleanly onto both Huawei Ascend and NVIDIA Hopper, making the precision profile hardware-agnostic in the practical sense. The first time someone said this aloud in a meeting, Chen looked up from his notebook with the expression I have come to associate with him noticing that two problems he had been treating as independent were the same problem.

We had the post-training pipeline that Su Yutong had been quietly redesigning since 2024 and that nobody else fully understood. I will come back to this.

What we did not have was a way to do attention at a million tokens that we believed in. Three candidate designs had been competing internally since the previous summer: a linear attention mechanism, a more aggressive MLA variant with deeper compression, and a hybrid sparse-and-compressed scheme that had been considered the weakest of the three. We did not, by mid-February, have a decision.

The first argument was about attention. The team had formed informally in the first week of January around a researcher I will call Jia, who is one of the people whose name is on the V4 paper as co-first-author and whose contribution I am beginning to feel a vague guilt about underrepresenting. Jia is approximately my age. She joined the lab six months after I did. Her work had been on the indexer mechanism in V3.2's DSA, and her interest in V4 was that she had spent the previous year persuaded the indexer was the right mechanism operating on the wrong representation. Her view, which she had been articulating since at least August, was that you needed to compress the KV cache along the sequence dimension first and then run the indexer on the compressed representation. Compress, then sparsely select — mathematically more efficient than either alone.

The argument against was made principally by a researcher I will call Wen, whose objection was about complexity. We were already shipping with a residual stream change, an optimizer change, a precision change, and a post-training redesign. Adding a fundamentally new attention mechanism on top was a risk profile the lab had never attempted in a single release. Wen wanted the more aggressive MLA variant — a modification of something we already understood. Wen was not wrong.

The argument lasted from the first week of January to roughly mid-February. Three formal meetings, approximately 20 informal conversations, and one 30-page document that Jia produced in late January and that I read twice and did not, the first time, fully understand. The resolution was not a single decision but a series of small ones that accumulated into a design.

The compression rate would be four — every four tokens of KV compressed into one entry, and the indexer selecting the top-k of these. Top-k of 1,024 for V4-Pro and 512 for V4-Flash. A 128-token uncompressed sliding window concatenated for local fidelity, because early experiments showed local recall degrading badly without it. Jia's mechanism — Compressed Sparse Attention, CSA — would alternate with a second mechanism, Heavily Compressed Attention, which compressed every 128 tokens into a single entry and ran dense attention over them. CSA layers and HCA layers through the network. Fine-grained sparse lookups alternating with global compressed sweeps at every depth.

The first ablations on a 200B configuration in mid-February were very good. Single-token FLOPs at million-token context were approximately 27% of what V3.2's mechanism would have required. KV cache footprint approximately 10%. Retrieval quality held at 94% recall at 128K and degraded to 66% at 1M — worse than we had hoped, better than any open model we knew about. We accepted the design on the 23rd. Jia took the next day off, which is the only time in 2.5 years I am aware of her doing so.

The second argument was about the residual stream, and it was won by Chen Wei, though the manner is more complicated than that sentence implies.

The hyper-connections technique we had been using since V3.2 — widening the residual stream by a factor of four — was producing signal amplification at trillion-parameter scale of approximately 3,000 times across the depth of the network. The gradients downstream became unreliable. The loss curves became noisy. The model trained but did not train well. We had known since November. We had not had a fix.

Chen does not work on problems in clean periods of focused effort. He works on them in long stretches of partial attention, during which he appears to be doing other things, and then produces a result that has the characteristic of having been arrived at slowly and from multiple angles. Through December he had been mentioning the residual stream at lunch in a way that suggested he was holding it while doing other things. He had read three older papers on doubly stochastic matrices that I had not known he was familiar with. He had, in early January, mentioned to me that he was thinking about whether Sinkhorn-Knopp normalization could constrain the residual mapping matrix in a way that bounded its spectral norm. I had not understood what he was talking about. He had not fully understood it himself.

The proof came in pieces over 11 days in early February. A sketch on a whiteboard while talking through the idea with Jia, who was in the room for a different reason and who, when she saw what Chen was drawing, sat down and started asking questions. A clean derivation over the following weekend showing that constraining the mapping matrix onto the Birkhoff polytope — the manifold of doubly stochastic matrices — bounded the spectral norm at one. And an empirical confirmation on a 200B ablation that we watched for a day and a half before anyone was willing to say it out loud.

The confirmation came on a Tuesday morning in mid-February. I was at my desk. Chen was at his. Su came through and stopped at the dashboard and looked at the loss curve for what I would estimate was 40 seconds. Then she said, quietly, 这是地基. This is the foundation. Then she went to the kitchen.

Chen's name is on the paper. He is the second author on the mHC section. The proof was not his alone — Jia asked the questions that pushed his first sketch toward its final form, and several others had been thinking about variants of the constraint approach. Authorship is not the same as origination, and origination is not the same as proof, and proof is not the same as making it work in production.

The third argument was the longest, about post-training, and was won by Su Yutong over the course of approximately 14 months. I will describe it in the next section, because it sits in the gap between the architecture and the training run.

By the third week of February, we had a model. Attention mechanism, residual stream, optimizer, precision profile, routing scheme, post-training pipeline. A 1.6 trillion parameter design that, on the basis of the ablations, we believed would train and would not collapse during the long-context warmup. We had a cluster reserved for us and a serving infrastructure being prepared in parallel. What we did not have was confidence. We had the feeling that comes after you have made a series of decisions you cannot easily reverse — you are no longer arguing about what to do and have begun the harder work of finding out whether what you decided will work. This is, in my experience of research, the worst part of any project. The arguments are over. The results are not yet in. The only thing you can do is wait.

The training run started on February 28.

* * *
IV

The Cluster

A training run is not a launch. The press describes it as a thing that succeeds or fails, the way a rocket succeeds or fails, with the events in between treated as an unbroken arc. A training run is more like a long voyage on a ship that has been built mostly correctly but that needs adjustment in dozens of small ways during the journey, staffed by people who take shifts at the wheel and at the engine and who, between shifts, sleep in bunks and eat food cooked in advance and emerge for their watches and go back below. The ship is mostly fine. The watches are mostly boring. The crew develops, over the course of the voyage, a relationship with the ship that is not romantic but is also not nothing.

The V4-Pro run started on February 28 at 03:14, which was the time the cluster reservation began and the time the on-call engineer — a woman I will call Dai — ran the initialization script. Most of us were not in the building. Dai watched the first 1,000 steps to confirm the loss was decreasing and the early-warning indicators were quiet. Then she sent a message to the cluster channel: 看起来正常 — looks normal — and went home.

The run was scheduled for 42 days. It would take 46. The first two weeks were uneventful. The sequence-length warmup ran from 4K to 16K to 64K. The Muon optimizer behaved as it had in the smaller runs. The mHC residual scheme held. The loss curve descended in the smooth, slightly noisy way that means the model is learning — the small daily wobbles that mean nothing and the broader weekly trend that means everything. I checked the dashboard on my phone in the evenings. Chen checked it more often. Su checked it less often than either of us, which I understood to be either confidence or fatalism and which I never asked her to clarify.

The unplanned pause came at step 470,000, on the morning of March 18, during the long-context phase at 1M sequence length.

I was in the building. It was a Wednesday. I had come in around eight for a code review at nine. The dashboard, when I opened it, showed a loss curve that had, in the previous 20 minutes, departed from its trajectory and begun to climb. The slope was not steep. It was unmistakable. I watched it for perhaps 90 seconds before I called Chen, who had been at home — the first time he had slept at home in 11 days.

He answered on the second ring.

Loss is climbing.

How fast?

Slow. About 0.02 per 1,000 steps for the last 20 minutes.

Is the indexer firing right?

I haven't checked.

Check.

I checked. The indexer was firing approximately as expected. Chen arrived at 8:47 in a taxi. Su arrived independently at 8:52, which surprised me — she had not been on call and had no reason to be in the building. She had seen the dashboard from her apartment and come in without anyone calling her.

The cluster bay is a long narrow room on the 11th floor with two rows of terminals facing each other and a wall of status monitors at the far end that cast the room in the blue-green light of dashboards that are always on. The air conditioning runs louder here than anywhere else in the building. Chen pulled a second chair to the main terminal and sat down next to me. Su stood behind us, one hand on the back of Chen's chair, watching the loss curve update every 30 seconds on the center monitor.

Show me the routing weights, Su said.

I pulled up the routing distribution. The histogram was wrong — not dramatically, but visibly. The expert selection had begun to cluster. Three of the 64 experts were receiving approximately twice the token load they should have been.

Since when, Chen said.

I don't know. The logging granularity on the router is per-1,000 steps.

Pull the last four checkpoints.

I pulled them. We compared the routing distributions across four checkpoints spanning the previous 8 hours. The clustering had begun at approximately step 468,000 — 2,000 steps before the loss curve had begun to show it. The router had been drifting for hours before the loss had noticed.

Chen leaned back. He was quiet for perhaps 30 seconds, which is a long time when a training run is climbing. Then he said, not to either of us specifically, The router is updating on features that are already stale by the time the update lands.

Su said, Anticipatory Routing.

It was not a question. Anticipatory Routing was the scheme we had designed for exactly this failure mode — decoupling routing-network updates from the backbone by one step so that when a spike was detected the routing indices used historical parameters while features used current weights. The scheme was configured to engage automatically when the loss derivative crossed a threshold. The threshold was conservative. The first 20 minutes of the climb had not triggered it.

Engage it manually, Su said.

Now? I said. We haven't finished the diagnosis.

The diagnosis is the router. Engage it.

I looked at Chen. He nodded once. I engaged it at 12:14.

We sat in the cluster bay for the next four hours. Chen brought back three cups of tea from the kitchen upstairs. Nobody ate lunch. Su did not sit down once. She stood behind us with her arms crossed and watched the loss curve the way you watch a thing that is either going to turn around or is not going to turn around and that you cannot, at this point, do anything more to help. The loss climb slowed within 400 steps. It reversed within 900. By the time we left the cluster bay at four in the afternoon, the curve was back on its previous trajectory, and the routing distribution had spread back toward uniform, and the room smelled faintly of the green tea that Chen had spilled on the floor at some point without either of us noticing.

We paused the run the next day at noon for a configuration review. 36 hours of inspection — Chen and two infrastructure engineers on the routing weights, Su providing the post-training perspective, Jia providing the attention-mechanism perspective. The review concluded the fix had held, the spike had been a real instance of the failure mode the scheme was designed for, and the run could continue. We resumed at midnight on the 21st.

The line in the technical report — the underlying principles remain insufficiently understood — refers, among other things, to this episode. We do not have a clean theoretical account of why Anticipatory Routing works where it works, or how to predict the spike threshold from first principles. We argued, the week before submission, about whether to print the line. The argument was about whether it would be read as honesty or weakness. I was on the side that thought weakness. I was wrong. The line is in the paper.

The base model finished training on April 13. The on-call engineer checked the final loss, ran the evaluation suite, confirmed the numbers matched the trajectory, and posted: 训练完成. Training complete. There was no celebration. The base model was a base model. The model that would ship in 11 days was not yet built.

Su Yutong's contribution is the breakthrough of V4 that I think will travel furthest from this paper.

She had been working on the problem since the second half of 2024. Mixed reinforcement learning on a unified policy — the approach V3.2 had used, training a single model with rewards from multiple domains simultaneously — produced gradients that fought each other when the domains had different reward structures. The field had been observing this in different forms since 2023. What was new was Su's proposal, which she had been developing in small weekly meetings that most of the lab did not attend and that I had begun attending in October when she asked me, in passing, whether I would like to.

The proposal was decomposition followed by recomposition. Train N specialists independently, each with its own SFT corpus and GRPO loop and domain-specific reward model. The domains were math, competitive coding, agent use, instruction following, and a fifth category we called others. Each specialist produced a policy that was extremely good at its domain and was not asked to be good at anything else. Then a unified student, initialized from the base model, would be trained through on-policy rollouts to match the specialists' logit distributions through a weighted sum of full-vocabulary KL divergences.

The crucial detail — the one that took Su three months to convince the team of — was that the KL had to be computed over the full vocabulary, not estimated at the token level. When multiple teachers disagree, token-level KL estimates oscillate. The variance of the estimate dominates the signal, and the student's gradient reflects sampling noise rather than actual divergence. Full-vocabulary KL is more expensive but the gradient is the gradient. The student inherits not by averaging the specialists' outputs but by being pulled, in distribution space, toward each of them simultaneously.

The specialist training began in late February, running in parallel with the base model. The math specialist, in its first three weeks of GRPO training, drifted into a regime in which it was solving problems by writing long chains of reasoning that looked plausible at every step except one. The chains would proceed — axiom, inference, inference, inference — and somewhere in the middle, usually around the fourth or fifth step, there would be a substitution that did not follow from anything before it. The step was not random. It was the kind of step that a human reader would nod past if reading quickly, because the notation was correct and the logical connective was correct and only the content was wrong. We spent a week diagnosing what turned out to be a calibration problem in the verifier scoring the rollouts. It was, of all the problems we encountered in the specialist stage, the one that most made me think about what these models are actually doing when they reason.

The OPD merge began on the fifth of April. It ran for nine days.

Su was, during those nine days, the most focused I have ever seen another person be. She slept in the small conference room on a folding mattress left over from when the Huawei engineers had been there. She took meals at her desk — rice from the canteen downstairs, brought up in a plastic container that she ate from without looking at, her eyes on the terminal. She did not attend meetings. The rest of us learned, by the second day, to leave her alone. I would come in at eight in the morning and she would be at the terminal in the same position she had been in when I left at seven the night before, and I would not be entirely certain she had moved.

What she was doing, in those nine days, was tuning the KL weights across the five specialists while monitoring the student's rollout quality on held-out sets from each domain. The weights were not fixed. They shifted over the course of the merge as the student's competence in each domain changed. A weight that was correct on day two might overcorrect by day five because the student had absorbed the math specialist faster than the agent specialist and was now being pulled too hard toward a domain it had already learned. Su was adjusting the weights by hand, twice a day, based on her reading of the rollout distributions. There was no automation for this. There was Su, and the terminal, and her judgment about what the distributions should look like at each stage of the merge.

On the morning of April 14, the merge produced a model. We evaluated it against our internal benchmark suite that afternoon. The numbers came back through the day.

Codeforces internal Elo: 3,206. LiveCodeBench: 93.5. Putnam-2025: 12 out of 12, no failures. MMLU-Pro: 87.5. SWE-Bench Verified: 80.6. Toolathlon: 51.8.

I ran the Codeforces evaluation three times. Each time it returned the same number. Chen ran it twice more on his own machine. The number meant, if our methodology was correct, that V4-Pro at maximum reasoning effort was performing at approximately the 23rd rank among human contest participants. We had expected the model to be strong on competitive coding. We had not expected this. Chen sat at his desk for a long time after the third confirmation. He did not say anything.

Su looked at the numbers when they were posted to the channel. She wrote, in response, a single character: 嗯. Then she went back to work, because the merge was complete but the calibration of the reasoning-effort modes was not, and there were five more days before the model was ready to freeze.

SimpleQA-Verified came back at 57.9. A number we were proud of and which was also 17.5 points lower than Gemini 3.1 Pro. We had, at various points in the last two weeks, talked ourselves into and out of being satisfied with it. The numbers were what we had. They were good. They were not the best on every dimension. They were, on the dimensions where we were best, the best in the world.

The model froze on the 19th. The final evaluation suite ran on the 20th and 21st. The communications team began their pre-launch sequence on the 22nd. The Huawei team confirmed day-zero readiness on the 23rd. On the 24th, at 09:20, the repository flipped public, and I sent Chen a message, and he asked whether I had eaten breakfast.

V

The Week the World Arrived

The week before V4 launched, the world arrived in a sequence that is part of what made the week feel the way it felt.

April 16, a Wednesday. Anthropic shipped Claude Opus 4.7. The release came at approximately one in the morning Beijing time, which meant I was asleep and learned about it from the lab Slack at seven, by which time the numbers had been circulating for six hours. The number that mattered was SWE-Bench Pro: 64.3 — approximately 9 points higher than V4-Pro's internal score on the same benchmark. Opus 4.7 was, in a way we had been bracing for since the leaked reports in early April, the model that had pulled further ahead on the dimension we had most wanted to close on.

Chen came to my desk around nine. He sat down and said, in the flat tone he uses when suppressing a reaction, 他们也在跑. They are also running. I said, 我知道. The lab got quieter for the rest of that day. Not in any way an outsider would have noticed.

April 17, a Thursday. The Information published a report that DeepSeek was in talks for its first external fundraising round — at least $300 million at a valuation of at least $10 billion — and that the company had previously refused outside capital for the entire 2.5 years of its existence.

I learned about it from my cousin in Shanghai, who works in finance and reads The Information the way he reads weather forecasts. 看到了吗 — did you see it — and a link. The article described the talks as ongoing. It noted the founder had personally rejected approaches from approximately two dozen investors over 18 months, and that the change in posture had been the result of a private decision in February that had not been explained to the broader team.

I had not been told there had been a private decision in February. The first I had heard were rumors in early March that had passed between desks in the elliptical way these rumors always pass. The rumors had, in retrospect, been the early surfacing of a decision already made. I sat with this at my desk for longer than I would have expected. The feeling was not anger. It was something quieter — the recognition that I had been, through the soft membrane of internal communication that the lab maintains, the last category of person to learn about a thing that would change what the lab was. I had spent 18 months doing work I believed in for an organization whose shape I understood. The shape was changing, and the change had been decided without me, which was appropriate — I was not senior enough to be in that room — and which was also the kind of thing you can know is appropriate and still feel the weight of.

The lab Slack did not address the report. The communications team did not address it. The founder did not address it. Su came to my desk at four. She said, 看到了. I said, 我也看到了. She nodded and stood for a moment longer and went back to her desk. Six words. I have thought about them more often than I would have predicted.

April 23, a Wednesday. OpenAI shipped GPT-5.5. The number that mattered was Terminal-Bench 2.0: 82.7 — approximately 15 points higher than V4-Pro's internal score. On agentic coding, GPT-5.5 had moved into a tier no other model was operating in. The press coverage of V4 would now position our numbers against GPT-5.5's rather than Opus 4.6's, which was the comparison we had been preparing for.

Chen said at lunch, 他们的模型也是模型. Their model is also a model. I understood him to mean that GPT-5.5 was a piece of work done by people doing their work, and that the comparison would land where it landed. He was not wrong. I also did not, eating my noodles next to him, find this particularly comforting.

The same Wednesday, Michael Kratsios gave a speech in Washington accusing Chinese AI labs of industrial-scale IP theft. Beijing's Foreign Ministry called the accusations groundless by evening. On our floor, the speech did not produce any particular reaction. We had been the subject of similar accusations for 14 months and had stopped responding to the political theatre in late 2025. We noted it and continued the work.

The Opus 4.7 launch, the fundraising report, GPT-5.5, the Kratsios speech — taken together they formed a week in which the surrounding context of our launch had become unusually loud. We had hoped to ship into a quieter week. We were not going to.

I described the morning of April 24 in Section I. The Hugging Face repository went public at 09:20. The Huawei livestream began at 09:47, watched by approximately 400,000 people in its first hour. The engineers demonstrated V4-Pro running on an Ascend 950 SuperPod with low-latency inference. The model-chip-cloud closed loop, which the Chinese press had been writing about for a year, locked into the narrative the way a long-anticipated thing locks in when it finally arrives.

The Hacker News thread reached the front page within 30 minutes. The top comment was about the absence of CUDA dependency in the Huawei serving stack. The second was a technical critique of our long-context recall numbers that was, in my judgment, partly correct. I do not respond to Hacker News.

Simon Willison published his post at approximately 12:30 our time: DeepSeek V4 — almost on the frontier, a fraction of the price. The phrasing captured both the strength and the limitation in the same sentence. We were almost on the frontier. On the dimensions where we were strongest, on it. On the dimensions where we were not — and the week's releases meant there were more such dimensions than a week earlier — we were close, and we were cheap, and we were open, and we were not first.

The Chinese press ran variations of a single thesis: V4 had reframed million-token context as universal infrastructure, the model-chip-cloud loop had closed, and the next phase of the race would be played on different ground. 财经 ran a longer piece that asked whether V4 was the consolidation of DeepSeek's lead in Chinese open-source or the beginning of its decline. The piece was honest.

On the lab Slack, Chen posted a screenshot of the Hacker News top comment with the single character 嗯. Jia posted a longer reaction to a technical critique on Twitter that she was, I could tell, somewhat enjoying. Su did not post anything. She came to my desk near six and said, 休息一下, take a rest, and went home.

The founder posted, at approximately six in the evening, two characters in the team channel: 谢谢. Thank you. The channel was quiet for about a minute and resumed its slow accumulation of screenshots and reactions, and the message scrolled up along with everything else.

The launch announcement closed with a line from Xunzi's Fei Shi Er Zi:

不诱于誉，不恐于诽，率道而行，端然正己.

I read it on the metro home, on my phone, sitting near the door of the Number Three line. I did not gloss it for myself. It is a line from a text written approximately 2,300 years ago. Whatever it meant in the announcement was what it meant. I closed the phone and watched the city move past the window in the order I have learned to expect.

VI

率道而行

I get off the train at 19:14. The air is the air that arrives in Hangzhou in late April — a city that has decided it is no longer winter and has not yet committed to summer. The locust trees by the metro entrance have just begun to flower.

The walk from the metro to my building is six minutes by the direct route and twelve past the small park with the koi pond. Tonight I take the long way. The park is mostly empty. An old man on a bench with a thermos and a newspaper. A younger man walking a small dog that has the resigned expression of small dogs walked too often. The koi are at the surface, mouthing at insects I cannot see.

I sit on a bench near the pond for perhaps 10 minutes. I do not look at my phone. The thing about a long project ending is that it does not feel like the thing the project has been building toward. It feels like an ordinary evening with ordinary fatigue in which a particular pressure that has been present for a long time is now absent, and the absence is not immediately replaced by anything.

I let myself into the apartment. I am hungry — I have not had a real meal since a steamed bun from the convenience store at 9:30, which does not count. There is a small noodle place at the corner of my street that stays open until 11. I have been going to it for four years. The owner is a woman from Shanxi who makes the noodles by hand and who has, over four years, stopped asking me what I want.

The place is half full. She nods at me and points at the corner table. A few minutes later she brings me a bowl of biang biang noodles with chili oil and a small dish of pickled cucumber and a pot of tea I did not order. I eat slowly. The chili oil is one of the small reasons I have not moved.

My phone buzzes on the table. The notification is from Hugging Face: DeepSeek-V4-Pro has crossed 10,000 downloads. Twelve hours after launch. I look at the number for a moment and put the phone face-down.

It buzzes again 30 seconds later. A number I do not recognize. English. Hi, I'm reaching out from a major AI lab, and we have an opportunity I'd love to discuss. I read the first sentence and do not read the rest. I set the phone face-down and pour myself another cup of tea.

I think about Guo Daya, who is somewhere in Beijing tonight doing work that is good work, done by a person worth what he is being paid. I feel about him the way you feel about people who used to sit near you and have moved on — fond, a little distant, not particularly resolved.

I think about the work still ahead. The V5 we have not begun. The fundraise that may close at $10 billion or a different number or fall apart. The Ascend supernodes that will ship at scale in the second half of the year. The next Anthropic release and the next OpenAI release and the next argument about whether to ship something new or consolidate what we have. None of this is settled. The verdict on V4 will not be available for at least a year.

The path is the path.

I finish the noodles. I pay. I leave a small tip the owner has many times told me she does not require. I walk home.

Through the kitchen window the haze has cleared, and in the apartments across the courtyard I can see the small shapes of other people in their kitchens at this hour. The model is out. The work continues tomorrow.

I wash the glass. I turn off the kitchen light. I go to bed.
